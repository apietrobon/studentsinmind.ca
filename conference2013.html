Conference 2014

The inaugural Students in Mind conference.

Note: this information is for last year's conference! The preparation for the next conference is currently in the works.
Event Registration Online for Students in Mind Mental Health Conference powered by Eventbrite

As students caught up in the demands of university life, we often overlook mental health as an integral component of our well-being. Student mental health is a conversation that needs to take place across our campus, and it starts with Students in Mind, the first student-run conference on mental health at McGill. If mental health has touched your life or sparked your interest in any way, we invite you to join us on October 5th, 2013 for a dynamic conversation on issues ranging from self-care and stigma to peer support and advocacy.

Students in Mind is open to and intended for all McGill students. The conference will take place in the SSMU building, and there is a $5 registration fee for all attendees, which includes lunch.

Schedule

8:30-9:00 Registration

Check in and pick up your name tag, lunch ticket and optional conference package.

9:00-10:00 Welcome and Opening Keynote

Jana Luker, Executive Director of Student Services at McGill, will welcome conference attendees and offer opening remarks to launch the day's activities.
Dr. Joseph Rochford, our opening keynote speaker, will provide an introduction to the basics of mental health, specifically addressing on the associated stigmas and myths. As a professor in Psychiatry at McGill and a director of various programs, he will hone in on current trends in mental health on our campus.

10:00-10:15 Break

Coffee and snacks will be provided.

10:15-11:30 Workshop 1: Self-Care

As a registered attendee, you will have the opportunity to attend two workshops on the day of the conference. During these sessions you will gain the knowledge and skills to manage your personal mental health, support peers who may be dealing with mental health problems, and potentially get involved in mental health initiatives both on- and off-campus. Our workshops have developed in collaboration with existing McGill student services: Counselling Services, Mental Health Services, the Office for Students with Disabilities, and Student Health Services. The workshops will centre on the two themes of self-care and peer support.

Self-Care Workshop

The self-care workshop will focus on mental health education and specific self-care methods. You will:
  • strengthen your knowledge of mental health basics
  • learn about resourcecs available both at McGill and in Montreal
  • discover essential methods (e.g. stress management) for caring for your own mental health amid the demands of university life
  • develop your personal self-care plan
It is our hope that, through this workshop, you will leave the Students in Mind conference with tangible skills to promote mental wellness for yourself and amongst your peers!

11:30-12:45 Lunch

A buffet style lunch will be provided. Vegetarian options will be available. Several optional activities will be offered during the lunch period.
  • Club Fair: Various clubs and organizations related to mental health will be tabling during the lunch period. Attendees can learn more about getting involved with mental health initiatives at McGill and around Montreal.
  • Meditation Sitting: An introduction to using mindfulness practice to cultivate attention, awareness and compassion.
  • Laughter Yoga: Offered by Happy McGill.

12:45-2:00 Panel Discussion: A Mentally Healthy Campus

"What does a mentally healthy campus look like?" Prepare to engage in discussions aimed at answering this question! You will have the chance to participate in one of three panels, each consisting of panelists from a wide range of backgrounds, including advocates, student leaders, representatives from mental health organizations, individuals with lived experience, and mental health care professionals from McGill student services and the greater Montreal community. You will have the opportunity to indicate your order of preference for the three panels upon conference registration; all attendees will be assigned to panels on a first-come, first-served basis.

Designing a Supportive Campus Environment

This panel will examine the environmental factors that affect student mental health and well-being. It will also directly address how to build a more supportive and inclusive campus environment that facilitates connection and meaningful engagement inside and outside the classroom

  1. Dr. Ridha Joober, Researcher at the Douglas Institute of Mental Health and Professor in the Department of Psychiatry;
  2. Sara Houshmand, Former Post-Graduate Students' Society (PGSS) Equity Commissioner and a PhD student in Counselling Psychology at McGill
  3. Keven Joyal-Desmarais, Anxiety Support and Awareness Community (ASAC), Association for Well-Being and Enrichment (AWE), and Recent Graduate with an Honours Bachelor's in Psychology at McGill
  4. Dr. Nancy Low, Clinician-researcher in the Mood Disorders Program of the MUHC and Assistant Professor in the Department of Psychiatry at McGill University

Building Campus Resources

This panel will examine current Mental Health Services on campus, discuss ways in which these services can be made more accessible to students, and define its unique role among the range of other services - including other campus, peer-run and community initiatives.

  1. Dr. Robert Franck, Clinical Director of McGill's Student Mental Health Services;
  2. Dr. David Dunkley, Professor in the Department of Psychiatry and Researcher at the Jewish General Hospital;
  3. Katrina Bartellas, Co-Founder of the McGill Chapter of Unleash the Noise and Mental Health Student Advocate;
  4. Emily Yung, Post-Graduate Students' Society (PGSS) Health and Wellness Committee and Chair of the Peer Support Network

Advocating Mental Health on Campus

This panel will discuss strategies to raise awareness about mental health on campus, including the reduction of stigma and discrimination among all students, faculty, and staff.

  1. Sarah Berry, Course Instructor at McGill and Research Associate for the Opening Minds Anti-Stigma Initiative of the Mental Health Commission of Canada;
  2. Liette Desjardins, Psychiatric Nurse at the Douglas Institute of Mental Health;
  3. Patricia Lemoine, Vice-President on the Board of the Montreal Walks for Mental Health Foundation and Mental Health Advocate;
  4. Elizabeth Cawley, Post-Graduate Students' Society (PGSS) Health and Wellness Committee and PGSS Member Services Officer

2:00-2:15 Break

Coffee and snacks will be provided.

2:15-3:30 Workshop 2: Peer Support

As a registered attendee, you will have the opportunity to attend two workshops on the day of the conference. During these sessions you will gain the knowledge and skills to manage your personal mental health, support peers who may be dealing with mental health problems, and potentially get involved in mental health initiatives both on- and off-campus. Our workshops have developed in collaboration with existing McGill student services: Counselling Services, Mental Health Services, the Office for Students with Disabilities, and Student Health Services. The workshops will centre on the two themes of self-care and peer support.

Peer Support Workshop

The peer support workshop will train you to engage with your peers in a way that encourages both help-seeking and helping behaviour. You will:
  • gain new support skills and reinforce existing ones
  • be challenged to apply new skills in real-life scenarios
  • learn how to talk to a peer in distress and develop active listening skills
  • define the mental health needs of your campus and brainstorm potential solutions to these needs
It is our hope that, through this workshop, you will leave the Students in Mind conference with tangible skills to promote mental wellness for yourself and amongst your peers!

3:30-4:30 Closing Keynote and Closing Remarks

Dr. Camillo Zacchia, our closing keynote speaker, will provide his musings on anxiety, stigma, and the overall mental health of students. He will highlight future steps to prioritize your own mental health and advocate for the mental health of your peers and the campus!

Katrina Bartellas

As the co-founder of the McGill Chapter of Unleash the Noise, Katrina welcomes students to speak with her throughout the day about opportunities to get involved on campus. Apart from scholastic endeavors, Katrina enjoys running, playing music and travelling. Katrina has been a speaker with AMI Quebec, Unleash the Noise 2013, and Opening Minds. She is very happy to be involved with Students in Mind and commends the executive team for all their hard work in organizing such an important event at McGill.Check in and pick up your name tag, lunch ticket and optional conference package.

Sarah Berry

Sarah Berry is a PhD Candidate and Course Instructor in the Departments of Sociology and Social Studies of Medicine at McGill, and a Research Associate with the Mental Health Commission of Canada and the Douglas Mental Health University Institute. She is engaged in mental health advocacy and social justice work in the Montreal community, and is currently President of the Board of Directors of the Centre de jour St-James, a non-profit organization that provides services to persons who are marginalized and homeless in Montreal.

Elizabeth Cawley

Elizabeth is a PhD student at McGill University in the Department of Psychiatry. Her PhD work focuses on drug addiction and the role of stress in the relapse or exacerbation of mental illness. During her spare time Elizabeth has been involved in mental health awareness and advocacy at McGill for the past four years. As Member Services Officer at the Post Graduate Student's Society (PGSS) of McGill University since 2012 she has continually made mental health issues a priority and has sat on the Committee on Student Services, the Provost's Workgroup on Student Services, McGill Health Services Advisory Board, McGill Mental Health Services Advisory Board, McGill Counseling Services Advisory Board and PGSS Health and Wellness Committee. As an active member of the McGill community Elizabeth has worked to a) eliminate a service differential for graduate students at Mental Health Services decreasing wait time and ensuring equal access to mental health professions, b) aid in the development of an emergency screening package at Mental Health Services to ensure prompt attention to emergency situations and c) create greater awareness among graduate students of mental health issues and develop preventative health workshops and outreach for graduate students.

Liette Desjardins

Liette Desjardins has been a clinical nurse at the Douglas Mental Health University Institute for the past 30 years. After working for 25 years in emergency psychiatric care, she decided to try something new and started working at the Crossroads, the Douglas' day hospital. Here, Liette experiences the power of group-based support in the recovery process on a daily basis. She has many interests and her blog, "Soigner entre les lignes," was published this year by Éditions Multimondes, an associate of the news agency l'Agence Science Presse, as one of the best francophone scientific blogs. Liette hopes to reach out to others through creativity and aims to harness people's abilities to both help others and be helped, as she believes these to be great forces of change. Moreover, she sees her clinical practice as a creative process; in addition to a Bachelor's degree in nursing, she has also received academic training in creativity and creative writing. Liette also trains nurses throughout the province in emergency triage for individuals living with mental health problems.

David Dunkley

David Dunkley, Ph.D., is a Researcher with the Lady Davis Institute and SMBD Jewish General Hospital, and an Assistant Professor of Psychiatry and Associate Member of Psychology at McGill. He was trained in clinical psychology at McGill University, receiving his Ph.D. in 2001. He then pursued postdoctoral training in longitudinal diagnostic assessment of psychiatric disorders at Yale University from 2001-2003, followed by a postdoctoral fellowship in psychotherapy studies in depression at the Institute of Community and Family Psychiatry at the Jewish General Hospital (ICFP-JGH) from 2003-2004. The primary goal of Dr. Dunkley's research has been to examine the mechanisms through which perfectionism is a personality vulnerability factor to depression. He has received numerous fellowships and investigator awards, obtained grants from federal and provincial agencies, and has published several articles in respected journals using structural equation modeling/path analysis and multilevel modeling as the primary data analytic techniques. In 2005, he received the Canadian Psychological Association President's New Researcher Award.

Robert Franck

Dr. Robert Franck has worked at the McGill University Health Center Department of Psychiatry in out patient settings for over 30 years. For the past 15 years, he has also worked at McGill Mental Health Services, serving as the Clinical Director in the past 5 years. He has a strong interest in short term psychotherapies, especially psychodynamic and cognitive behavioural therapies.

Sara Houshmand

Sara Houshmand is a doctoral student in Counselling Psychology and has previously served as Equity Commissioner of McGill's Post Graduate Students' Society. Sara received McGill's inaugural Award for Equity and Community Building in the student category for her commitment to diversity and inclusivity. Her research interests pertain to multicultural counselling psychology and social justice in higher education.

Ridha Joober

Afflicting one percent of the adult population with devastating hallucinations, delusions and social impairment, schizophrenia has long been known to have a genetic component. Ridha Joober, MD, PhD, joined the Douglas Institute in 1999 and has since made significant contributions in unveiling the genetic basis of schizophrenia and ADHD. As a major research focus, Ridha Joober investigates the relationship between genetic variations and neuropsychological and behavioural traits in patients with schizophrenia and ADHD as well as the response of these disorders to pharmacological treatment. Ridha Joober's recent work has focused on the environmental factors involved in ADHD, particularly maternal smoking during pregnancy. This research has led to the development of more precise pharmacogenetic and environmental profiles of schizophrenia and ADHD that may ultimately lead to the earlier diagnosis and more effective treatment of these psychiatric disorders. Throughout his career, Ridha Joober has received a number of awards and distinctions, including the 2002 Young Investigator Award from the Canadian Institutes of Health Research (CIHR), a Senior Research Scholar Award from the Fonds de recherche en santé du Québec (FRSQ), and the Sam Lal Award from the Graham Boekh Foundation and the Canadian College of Neuropsychopharmacology (CCNP). Dr. Joober is also a member of the steering group based in Montreal for the Transformational Research in Adolescent Mental Health (TRAM), a call that has gone out to all service providers and scientists in Canada to create a transformation of mental health services for youth (11-25 years). This TRAM steering group is seeking your full participation to collectively plan and shape this initiative in the interest of achieving a much needed revolution in the way youth mental health services are delivered.

Keven Joyal-Desmarais

Keven is a recent graduate from the Honours Psychology program at McGill University, and is passionate about promoting psychological well-being and encouraging collaboration between people. Having both personal and research experience in depression, he volunteered with the Anxiety Support and Awareness Community, a group providing peer support to students suffering from mental health conditions at McGill. Later, he completed an internship at the Counselling Center of Vanier College, where he played a key role in designing and implementing a college-wide intervention against sexual assault. Having a keen interest in research, he recently joined the Psychosocial Oncology Lab at the Jewish General Hospital, where he assists studies that look at the factors that influence people's decisions to vaccinate against the Human Papillomavirus. With a passion in applying psychological findings, Keven continuously seeks to incorporate research on influence and decision-making in order to promote not only psychological well-being, but also the development of supportive and collaborative communities. He recently founded the Association for Well-Being and Enrichment, an initiative promoting collaboration between mental health groups in order to provide a more unified resource for students struggling with mental health and other difficulties.

Patricia Lemoine

Patricia is a bilingual mental health advocate and proud eating disorder survivor using her personal experience as a springboard to talk openly about mental illness, in order to end stigma and promote a dialogue around the issue. Initially trained as a lawyer, Patricia is an experienced public health communications and media professional currently working for the Cavendish Health and Social Services Centre. She is also Communications Director for the 2013 inaugural edition of Au Contraire Film Festival: Quebec's first film festival on mental illness, mental health and recovery. Additionally, she serves on the Board of the Montreal Walks for Mental Health Foundation as Vice-President, and is a Board member of TRACOM, a mental health crisis non-profit organization for adults. In 2013, Patricia joined the Collateral Damage: Left Behind by Suicide's advisory team in order to support this unique initiative. Patricia is also co-author of the Surviving ED blog on HealthyPlace.com, America's Mental Health Channel. When she isn't advocating for mental health initiatives, giving peer support to people suffering from eating disorders or speaking publicly about her experience, Patricia can be found in downward dog, swimming or writing while humming to Leonard Cohen.

Nancy Low

Dr. Low is a clinician-researcher in the Mood Disorders Program of the MUHC and Assistant Professor in the Department of Psychiatry at McGill University. She is a psychiatrist with training in psychiatric and genetic epidemiology and interested in familial risk in psychiatric disorders. Her research focuses on mood and anxiety disorders and other complex, chronic disorders. She uses (a) family and (b) longitudinal cohort study approaches to characterize (1) the phenotypic heterogeneity of what is transmitted within families; (2) the factors that contribute to the familial transmission, such as other psychiatric comorbidity, genetic factors, and the source of subject sampling; and (3) the role of environmental exposures on the course, progress, and outcomes of illness. Dr. Low also uses (4) comorbid disorders (e.g., migraine, sleep, cardiovascular diseases) to derive and evaluate possible familial phenotypes of psychiatric disorders.

Joseph Rochford

Advances in our understanding of environmental influences and molecular biology on neuronal and psychological development have allowed for the creation of more sophisticated and valid models of neurological and psychiatric disorders. Joseph Rochford, PhD and his team in the Neurophenotyping Centre provide consultation and phenotyping services to all members of the scientific community of Quebec and Canada interested in assessing the behavioural impact of genes, the environment, and their interaction. Joseph Rochford is also actively involved in educational and training initiatives. He is the University Associate Director of the Integrated Program in Neuroscience at McGill and is involved with Brain Awareness Week.

Emily Yung

Emily Yung, who completed her Bachelor's in Health Sciences from the University of Western Ontario, is now beginning her second year as a Master's student in the Department of Psychiatry at McGill. She previously served as a member of the McGill Mental Health Services Advisory Board and is currently the Health and Wellness Commissioner of the Post-Graduate Students' Society. She is also working with a team of undergraduate and graduate students as well as Student Services to create the Peer Support Program at McGill. If you are interested in learning more about the Peer Support Program, please feel free to email her at health.pgss@mail.mcgill.ca.

Camillo Zacchia

Camillo Zacchia received a PhD in clinical psychology from McGill University in 1987. Today, as senior advisor for the Mental Health Education Office (MHEO), he coordinates and develops training programs for the general public and our partners. Camillo Zacchia is vice-chair of the board for the organization Phobies-Zéro and was chair of the Douglas Clinical Ethics Committee from 2003 to 2010. Camillo Zacchia is an occasional contributor to The Gazette and publishes a column in the Métro newspaper every second Tuesday. This paper is distributed free of charge to all Montréal metro users and 100 of his columns have been compiled into Parts of life, a book distributed in doctors' offices across Quebec. Camillo Zacchia also writes a blog Psychospeak with Dr. Z which brings together scientific knowledge, critical thinking, and common sense to the world around us.
