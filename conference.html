Conference 2014

The annual Students in Mind conference.

As students get caught up in the demands of university life, we often overlook mental health as an integral component of our well-being. Students in Mind is an annual mental health conference taking place at McGill. If mental health has touched your life or sparked your interest in any way, we invite you to join us on October 5th, 2014 for a dynamic conversation on issues ranging from self-care and stigma to peer support and advocacy.

Students in Mind is open to and intended for all McGill students. The conference will take place in the SSMU building. There is a $6 on-line registration fee (or $5 if you get a ticket during tabling hours on campus). The fee is the same for all attendees and includes lunch, coffee and snacks.

10:00-11:15 Workshop 1: Self-Care

As a registered attendee, you will have the opportunity to attend two workshops on the day of the conference. During these sessions you will gain the knowledge and skills to manage your personal mental health, support peers who may be dealing with mental health problems, and potentially get involved in mental health initiatives both on- and off-campus. Our workshops have been developed in collaboration with existing McGill student services: Counselling Services, Mental Health Services, the Office for Students with Disabilities, and Student Health Services. The workshops will centre on the two themes of self-care and peer support.

Self-Care Workshop

The self-care workshop will focus on developing an awareness of one`s thoughts, emotions, beliefs, and behavior and work towards creating an individualized plan to maintain and improve self-care. Attendees will:
  • Assess their current level of self-care
  • Become aware of their mind and body
  • Find ways to incorporate self-care into their daily lifestyle
It is our hope that, through this workshop, you will leave the Students in Mind conference with tangible skills to promote mental wellness for yourself and amongst your peers!

1:00-2:00 Panel Discussions

Mental health/Illness and the Criminality

Are people who have mental illnesses responsible for their actions? Alternatively, are people who commit unprompted murder ever mentally healthy? There is substantive evidence of the over-representation of people with various forms of mental illnesses in the criminal justice system. What are some processes that could be put into place that acknowledge and mitigate barriers for people with mental health disabilities? The broader purpose of this panel discussion is to mitigate the negative stigma surrounding mental illness.

  1. Sarah Berry, Course Instructor at McGill and Research Associate for the Opening Minds Anti-Stigma Initiative of the Mental Health Commission of Canada;
  2. Mark Anscombe, Vice-President External for McGill Students' Nightline;

Cross-Cultural Portrayals of Mental Health

This panel centers around themes that constitute the focus of research in transcultural psychiatry: the culture of diagnosis in North America, the perception of normality and the perception of happiness, and how to respond to diversity in mental health care. The discussion also revolves around the topic of psychiatric community care: To what extent does community acceptance, inclusion and the sense of belonging have a healing effect? How does perception of normality affect community acceptance and inclusion?.

  1. Dr. Ian Gold, Professor in the Philosophy Department and the Division of Social and Transcultural Psychiatry Department, McGill University; Canada Research Chair in Philosophy and Psychiatry;

The Impact of Social Media on Mood

How do media and social networks affect the mood? Scientists have suggested that negative posts on Facebook have a domino effect. However, the idea that there is a social desirability bias in Facebook posts prevails: people often tend to over-represent the positive aspects of their lives and under-represent the sad ones. In general, what is the impact of social media on people`s self-image and self-esteem? In this panel, the discussion will revolve around the various ways in which we can use social media as a platform for information and discussion about mental health, and how it can be used to promote and encourage healthier habits.

  1. Dr. Ridha Joober, Professor in the Department of Psychiatry, McGill University; Clinician Scientist, Douglas Mental Health University Institute; Director, Program for early intervention and prevention in psychotic disorders (PEPP-Montreal); Director, Psychosis and neurodevelopmental research Program, Douglas Research Centre; Co-Editor In Chief, Journal of psychiatry and neuroscience;
  2. Dr. Randi Fogelbaum, ( M.S.W., P.S.W., M.F.T.) / Director of the Eating Disorder Program at McGill University; Psychotherapist at McGill Mental Health Services; Social Worker and Individual, Couple and Family Therapist;

Strategies for Student Mental Health

What can we do to improve mental health on campus? What are ways in which we can build a more supportive and inclusive campus environment? Specifically, given that we are faced with high levels of anxiety and depression, what can we do to alleviate the stress placed on students? In this panel, we will discuss concrete strategies that can be implemented with an eye to creating a healthier campus (e.g. more interactive and diverse classroom experiences, flexible exam schedules, listing of services on McGill syllabi, etc.). We will also discuss the role of McGill Mental Health Services and Counselling services in building a more supportive campus, and how existing student-led initiatives fit in the spectrum of mental health care on campus.

  1. Tanja Beck, Access Services Advisor at the Office for Students with Disabilities (OSD);
  2. Elizabeth Cawley, PhD Candidate Psychiatry, McGill University / Member Services Officer at the Post Graduate Students' Society; Co-Founder/President at the Psychiatry Graduate Students' Association;
  3. Dr. Nancy Low, Clinician-researcher in the Mood Disorders Program of the MUHC and Assistant Professor in the Department of Psychiatry at McGill University

Peer Support Workshop

The peer support workshop will focus on ways to be present and listen effectively when communicating with another person, as well as how to incorporate these practices into one`s daily life and interpersonal relationships. Attendees will:

  • Develop effective strategies for listening
